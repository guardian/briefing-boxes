A short history of protectionism

There have been examples of trade protectionism throughout history, with records showing the Roman state prohibiting the cultivation of wine beyond the Alps and the ancient Greeks imposing duties of 5% at all ports in the empire.
Nations along the ancient silk road from Asia grew rich on taxes, enforced by military might, while the English wool trade with Flanders during the middle ages was among reasons behind ​the hundred years’ war​ with France. Edward III instructed his chancellor sit atop a woolsack to symbolise the importance of the commodity to medieval England – and remains the seat of the House of Lords speaker to this day.
The renaissance brought the gradual end of this period of mercantilism, pushed further forward by the free market principles developed by David Ricardo and Adam Smith during the 18th and 19th century. Before then, the nNavigation aAct forced goods arriving in Britain to come ashore onfrom British ships, while records as far back as the 12th century refer to special taxes on imports.
The touchstone moment came with the abolition of the Corn Laws in 1846. Grain shortages caused by Britain’s growing population and blockades during the Napoleonic Wwars meant prices were high. Upon pPeace in 1815 foreign imports could have pushed down the price of foreign imports, yet tariffs kept prices them high.
The push for repeal split the Conservative party between those on the side of landowners – in favour of the taxes – and the new generation of wealthy industrialists wanting to lower the price of bread for the burgeoning middle class. Robert Peel, the then prime minister, sided with the factory owners and spurred the move towards greater trade liberalisation across Europe.
The Wall Street crash of 1929 led to the passage of the Smoot-Hawley tariffs of 1930 in the US to protect domestic industrial production, although triggered tit-for-tat responses which economists believe contributed to the damaging length and depth of the Great Depression.
Following the second world war, the world’s biggest economies created the Bretton Woods institutions of the World Bank and the International Monetary Fund, as well as agreeing the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade in 1947 – which would become the forerunner to the modern World Trade Organisation.
Globalisation during the post-war years and more countries signing up to the WTO helped average import tariffs to fall from approximately 14% in 1952 to 3.9% in 2005. However, they are gradually beginning to go the other way. Should the US impose all of its threats, it would have the highest levels of trade tariffs for over 50 years.
